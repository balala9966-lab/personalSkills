# Six Months Of Learning Vim From Scratch

I started learning Vim in November after watching a coworker edit a thousand-line file in what felt like seconds. I am writing this in Vim. The road here was not straight.

## The first week was bad

Every keystroke felt like fighting the editor. I would press `i` to insert, type a paragraph, and then forget to press Escape before reaching for my mouse. The mouse would not work the way I expected it to. I would lose entire paragraphs and not know how to get them back.

I almost quit on day three.

## The trick that changed things

A friend told me to stop trying to memorize commands. Instead, every time I wanted to do something inefficient, I should pause and look up the Vim way to do it. Look it up once, do it badly, then move on. Don't drill.

After three weeks of this, my hands knew where most of the common motions lived. Not because I practiced them deliberately — because I had wanted them and looked them up in context.

## What I wish someone had told me

The plugin ecosystem is a trap when you start. You will be tempted to install a hundred plugins because they look like they would save time. They don't. They add complexity to a system you don't yet understand. Spend two months with vanilla Vim. Add plugins only when you can articulate, in one sentence, what problem they are solving.

## Six months in

I edit faster than I did with VS Code, but only on some kinds of work. Refactoring across many files is still slower for me in Vim than in an IDE with proper symbol indexing. I'm okay with that. The point of Vim was never to be the fastest at everything. It was to feel less like I was fighting the editor.
