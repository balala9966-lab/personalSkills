# Composition Patterns (ink-notes extended)

10 structural patterns for editorial hand-drawn illustrations. Each pattern is a *shape of understanding* — pick the one that matches what the article is doing at the insertion point.

These patterns are specific to the `ink-notes` style and its derivatives (hand-drawn editorial, product-sketch feel). They work best with The Tinkerer or a user-supplied IP as the active subject.

## The 10 Patterns

### 1. Threshold

The character crosses a boundary, membrane, door, checkpoint, or weather line. Use when the article describes a before/after transition, an entry condition, or a gating decision.

### 2. Split Path

One input branches into different consequences. Use when the article shows a decision point with diverging outcomes, or when a single action has multiple effects.

### 3. Loop

Inputs return as feedback, debt, learning, or compounding effects. Use when the article describes iteration, technical debt accumulation, flywheel effects, or learning cycles.

### 4. Compression

Many messy pieces are squeezed into one usable form. Use when the article describes synthesis, summarization, abstraction, or reducing complexity.

### 5. Translation

One format becomes another through an odd machine or ritual. Use when the article discusses format conversion, protocol bridging, or cross-domain mapping.

### 6. Balance

Competing forces are held in tension. Use when the article discusses tradeoffs, equilibria, or "both are true" situations.

### 7. Excavation

Hidden causes are dug out from under visible symptoms. Use when the article is about root-cause analysis, debugging, or uncovering hidden structure.

### 8. Relay

One role hands off to another through a fragile interface. Use when the article discusses handoffs, API boundaries, team interfaces, or sequential ownership.

### 9. Repair

The character fixes a leak, crack, knot, or broken connector. Use when the article discusses bug fixes, debt repayment, or restoring broken systems.

### 10. Mismatch

Two systems try to interlock but have incompatible shapes. Use when the article discusses integration failures, impedance mismatches, or compatibility problems.

## How To Invent The Metaphor

Start from the article's actual claim, not a generic concept:

1. **Identify the transformation**: before → after, input → output, noise → signal, promise → cost.
2. **Choose a physical action** that embodies it. The character must *do* the action, not narrate it.
3. **Add one strange but disciplined object relationship**. "Strange" means unexpected in context; "disciplined" means it still reads clearly at article width.
4. **Add only enough labels** to make the metaphor readable. If the image needs a paragraph of explanation, the metaphor failed.

## Anti-Repetition List

Do NOT repeatedly use these visual moves across images in the same article:

- Conveyor belts
- Pulling a single rope or cable
- Fish as reusable content
- Stamp / toolbox productivity metaphors
- "Pitfall path" diagrams
- Generic funnels
- Puzzle pieces
- Ladders and mountains
- Brain with gears
- Lightbulbs as "ideas"
- Scales (unless the article is literally about balance)
- Factory assembly lines
- Hourglass as "time"

Reuse a composition only when the user explicitly asks to mimic a reference.

## Keep It Editorial

The image should feel like a body illustration inserted between paragraphs. It should NOT feel like:

- A slide cover
- A process manual
- A SaaS landing-page hero
- A technical architecture diagram
- A children's book spread
- A stock illustration from a blog template

## Pattern × Article Signal

Quick reference for which pattern to reach for based on what the article is doing:

| Article is describing... | First-pick pattern |
|--------------------------|-------------------|
| A decision with consequences | Split Path |
| A system that feeds back into itself | Loop |
| A before/after state change | Threshold |
| Making something complex simple | Compression |
| Converting between formats/protocols | Translation |
| Two competing concerns | Balance |
| Finding the root cause | Excavation |
| A handoff between teams/systems | Relay |
| Fixing something broken | Repair |
| Two things that don't fit together | Mismatch |
